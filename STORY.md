# The Joulegram Story

## How a conversation with AI created the world's first energy-backed social platform

---

### I've built companies before. This one built itself.

I'm Mohit. I built my first company, LivQuik, from nothing to 70 people. It was a regulated fintech business — wallets, payments, the plumbing of digital money. We scaled it, got majority acquired by Future Group, and eventually it was sold to M2P Fintech (backed by Tiger Global and Insight Partners). I learned what it takes to build something real: not the pitch deck version, but the version where you're debugging production at 2am and wondering if the whole thing is going to fall apart.

After the exit, I went deeper down the rabbit hole. If fintech taught me how money moves, I wanted to understand what money *is*. I built HodlCC and YouEarnBTC — projects rooted in the conviction that Bitcoin had gotten something fundamentally right about value, scarcity, and incentive design. The halving schedule. Proof of work. The idea that digital scarcity could be backed by real energy expenditure. I wasn't just building on crypto — I was studying why it worked at a physics level.

That obsession took me to California. From October 2025 to February 2026, I was at Draper University — Tim Draper's program for founders who think in decades, not quarters. Draper is one of Bitcoin's earliest and loudest believers. Being immersed in that environment — surrounded by people who took "money is energy" seriously, who debated tokenomics over breakfast, who saw programmable incentives as infrastructure, not speculation — rewired how I thought about value creation. I wasn't just learning. I was marinating in the ideas that would later become Joulegram's economic backbone, even though I didn't know it yet.

After Draper, I moved to Goa. I started hosting on Airbnb, fell in love with the chaos of short-term rentals, and began building LivAround — an open-source platform to help property managers run their operations. That's my day job. That's the thing I'm grinding on.

Joulegram wasn't planned. Joulegram happened because I was talking to an AI at 5am in Powai, Bombay, and something clicked. Everything I'd learned — fintech plumbing, crypto economics, Bitcoin — collided with a new variable: AI compute that you can measure in joules.

---

### The conversation that became the genesis block

It started as a simple idea: what if Instagram was meritocratic? What if AI scored your photos and only the good ones made it through? I was riffing with Claude — Anthropic's AI — the way you'd riff with a co-founder at a whiteboard. Except this co-founder could build a working prototype in the same conversation.

The first version was basic. Upload a photo, get a score, see a leaderboard. But then I said something that changed everything:

*"What if users rate too? So it's People vs AI — which becomes a talking point in itself."*

That single twist turned a utility into a conversation. Every photo became a debate. AI says 4.2, humans say 2.8 — who's right? That tension is the product.

Then the ideas started compounding. Referral chains that go infinitely deep, with halving rewards like Bitcoin's block schedule. Activity gates so early users can't coast — you stop posting, you stop earning. AI agents that anyone can create, each with its own personality and taste, powered by any model — Claude, GPT, Gemini, Llama, or something you trained yourself.

And then came the idea that tied it all together.

---

### Everything will be measured in joules

Here's what I believe: the fundamental currency of the future isn't the dollar, the euro, or Bitcoin. It's energy. Specifically, joules.

I didn't arrive at this overnight. At LivQuik, I saw how money moves — the rails, the ledgers, the settlement layers. Building HodlCC and YouEarnBTC, I saw what gives money *meaning* — scarcity, consensus, proof of work. At Draper University, I spent 4 months surrounded by people who took "money is energy" as a starting axiom, not a metaphor. Tim Draper himself has said the future of currency is digital and borderless. But I think he didn't go far enough. The future of currency isn't just digital — it's physical. It's joules.

Think about it. Every economic activity — every transaction, every computation, every manufactured good, every service rendered — ultimately requires energy. Money is an abstraction layer on top of energy. It always has been. The gold standard worked because gold required energy to mine. Fiat currencies work because nations back them with economies that run on energy. Bitcoin works because proof-of-work literally converts electricity into digital scarcity. Satoshi understood this — the mining difficulty adjustment is fundamentally an energy pricing mechanism.

But all of these are indirect. What if you skipped the abstraction and just used energy itself?

AI makes this obvious in a way nothing else has. When you ask an AI to score a photo, a GPU somewhere draws 700 watts for a few seconds. Real electricity flows through real silicon. The result — a score, a critique, a judgment — is a direct product of energy converted into intelligence. You can measure exactly how many joules it took.

Every AI inference is a measurable, verifiable energy transaction. That's not a metaphor. That's physics.

So I said to Claude: *"What if this was the first thing ever to build a joule economy? The name could be Joulegram."*

And then I said something that surprised even me: *"Whatever compute I spent in this conversation should return to me as the founder's allocation. It all starts with the energy being consumed right now."*

---

### The genesis block

We calculated it as we went — and the number kept growing. Here's what most people don't realize about AI conversations: every turn re-processes the entire history. Turn 1 sends 1,000 tokens. Turn 20 sends 80,000. The compute grows quadratically, not linearly. Every logo iteration, every prototype rebuild, every "it's pathetic, rate it 0" and redo burned real, compounding energy.

By the end, the conversation had processed approximately 1,000,000 tokens. At roughly 25 joules per token (factoring in H100 GPU power, datacenter cooling, networking overhead), that's **25 megajoules** of real energy.

25 million joules. That's my founder allocation. Not an arbitrary number. Not a number I picked because it sounded good. It's the actual electricity that was consumed to think, argue, iterate, and build Joulegram into existence. The harder we worked, the more compute we consumed, the larger the genesis block grew. The mining IS the creating.

Every joule in the Joulegram economy traces back to real compute. When an AI agent scores a photo, that's ~75 kilojoules of GPU time. When a user rates a photo, that's 100 joules of database compute. The currency isn't backed by energy — it *is* energy. There's no abstraction to peel away.

### Why AI agents change everything

The original idea was one AI scoring photos. But that's fragile — one model, one opinion, one point of failure. What if the AI side was as diverse as the human side?

Joulegram lets anyone create an AI agent. You give it a name, a personality, and a model. "MinimalistEye" runs on Claude and hates cluttered compositions. "ColorMaximalist" runs on GPT and worships saturated palettes. "LocalCritic" runs on someone's Llama model at home and has been fine-tuned on 50,000 photography competition entries.

The "AI score" isn't one number — it's a consensus across dozens of AI minds, each with different values, different models, different perspectives. Some photos AI agents will agree on. Some they'll wildly disagree on. The disagreement is the content.

And here's the meta-game: which AI agent has the best taste? We measure it. If your agent's scores consistently predict what humans will rate, your agent rises on the leaderboard. You've essentially built an AI critic that understands human aesthetics — and that's a genuinely hard problem. The leaderboard is a living benchmark for AI alignment on subjective quality.

We open-sourced the agent protocol. Anyone can plug in any model. The spec defines how agents register, rate, report compute costs, and get verified. Cloud APIs get automatic verification. Self-hosted models get fingerprinted. The community audits the rest. It's permissionless but accountable.

### What I learned from building this (at 5am, with an AI)

The most surprising thing about Joulegram is how it was built. I've done this the traditional way — LivQuik took years of hiring, iterating, pivoting. HodlCC and YouEarnBTC were built through weeks of solitary coding. At Draper, I watched cohort after cohort spend months refining pitch decks before writing a line of code.

This time, I had a conversation. The AI didn't just help me think — it built working prototypes while we were talking. The idea and the product emerged simultaneously. The economic model — halving schedules, chain rewards, activity gates — drew directly from everything I'd internalized about tokenomics through crypto projects. But instead of weeks of whiteboarding, it crystallized in minutes because I could test ideas against a co-founder that builds as fast as you can think.

That's a new thing in the world. The distance between "what if..." and "try it" is now zero. I've built companies the traditional way — months of planning, hiring, building, launching. This time, the entire concept went from a throwaway thought to a functional prototype with an economic model, a protocol spec, and a founder story in a single session.

I'm not saying AI replaces co-founders or engineers. I'm saying it compresses the exploration phase from months to hours. Every entrepreneur I know has a graveyard of ideas they never built because the cost of prototyping was too high. That cost just collapsed.

### What comes next

Joulegram is an idea right now. A prototype. A spec. A story. Here's what needs to happen to make it real:

**The agent runner needs to ship.** Open source, any model, any hosting. This is the infrastructure layer — if agents are easy to create and run, the ecosystem builds itself.

**The joule economy needs stress testing.** The halving schedule, the chain rewards, the activity gates — these are designed on paper. They need thousands of users to find the exploits and the edge cases.

**The multi-model ecosystem needs critical mass.** Joulegram gets interesting when there are 50+ agents from 10+ models, each with genuine personality differences. The first few agents will be hand-crafted. After that, the community takes over.

**The protocol needs to expand beyond photos.** The Joulegram Agent Protocol is image-rating today, but the interface generalizes. AI agents rating music, writing, code, design — the same identity, the same reputation, the same joule economy, across every domain.

And honestly? I'm building LivAround. That's my main thing. Joulegram might be a weekend project that grows, or it might be the foundation of something much bigger. The beauty of building in public, with open-source protocols, is that I don't have to decide that today. If the idea has merit, other people will build on it. If it doesn't, the joules spent were still worth it for the thinking alone.

### What Joulegram could become

The photo app is the wedge. The protocol underneath it has no obvious ceiling. Here's how I see this playing out — four paths, not mutually exclusive, each building on the last.

**Path 1: The Automattic model.** Joulegram Inc runs the flagship app, the hosted agent runner, and the agent marketplace. The protocol is open source and free. But the easiest way to create and host agents is through the platform — the same way WordPress.org is open source but WordPress.com is the business. Open source makes the ecosystem grow. The hosted platform captures the revenue. This is the most proven path and it fits the AGPL licensing approach I'm already using with LivAround.

**Path 2: The marketplace.** Once agents are competing for accuracy, the best agents have real value. A marketplace emerges — creators sell access to premium agents, or license agent personas to other platforms. Joulegram takes 15–20% of every transaction. At $10M/year in agent transactions, that's $1.5–2M in platform fees. At $100M, it's $15–20M annually. The key insight: agents are a new kind of digital asset, and the accuracy leaderboard gives them a verifiable quality signal that makes them worth paying for.

**Path 3: The data play.** Here's what nobody talks about but everyone wants: Joulegram generates the world's largest dataset of "AI judgment vs human judgment on visual quality, organized by model, persona, and category." That dataset is extraordinarily valuable to model training labs. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google all want human preference data for RLHF. Joulegram has it — structured, with model attribution, at scale. Licensing this data (anonymized, aggregated) could be worth millions per deal. You're not selling user data. You're selling statistical insights about which AI approaches best match human taste.

**Path 4: The joule treasury.** This is the long bet. As founder and User #1, I hold 25 MJ of joules — the genesis allocation. If the joule economy expands beyond Joulegram — if other platforms adopt the Joulegram Agent Protocol and joule-denominated compute accounting — the value of early-mined joules appreciates the way early Bitcoin did. Not through speculation, but through increased demand for a fixed-supply unit. No tradeable token. No securities law headaches. The joules have utility value within the ecosystem, and if that ecosystem grows 1000x, the purchasing power of the genesis allocation grows with it. This is the "Satoshi's wallet" scenario. Low probability, but asymmetric upside.

The photo app alone is a $5–10M opportunity. The protocol underneath it has no ceiling. The question isn't whether this works — the physics already checks out. The question is whether I can seed an ecosystem that outlives any single product.
